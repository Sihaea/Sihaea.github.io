About


What is Flow?

Flow is a psychological state of optimal performance characterised by intense focus, seamless execution, and a distorted sense of time. Originating from the work of Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, flow is a proven enhancer of skill acquisition and creativity.

Project Outline:

The following project investigates how flow can be stimulated and applied in language learning through the combined use of a gameified language learning app, an adaptive deep learning model, and tDCS (transcranial direct current stimulation) technology. The goal is to create a system that dynamically responds to user metrics, sustaining flow and enhancing language acquisition.

Motivation


Why is flow relevant to language learning?

Flow has been shown to enhance cognitive performance and learning outcomes. Several modern studies have utilised tDCS to stimulate flow in the brain. A 2021 study found a 1.5 mA cathodal right and anodal left stimulation of the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex resulted in greater language recall in foreign language learning participants. (Bolling et al., 2021) Another successful study quantifying flow applied deep learning techniques to metrics obtained from students in an e-learning environment. (Semerci & Goularas, 2020)

The following research aims to bridge neuroscience and pedagogy and synthesise such studies by:



• Developing a language learning app designed to facilitate the characteristics of flow. • Developing an adaptive deep learning model that adjusts to learners' individual needs for entering flow in real-time. • Leveraging tDCS technology to facilitate quicker transitions from explicit to implicit knowledge.

Background


The Characteristics of Flow~

Flow is colloquially referred to as being 'in the zone' or, 'locked in', and several characteristics associated with the state have been identified (Nakamura & Csikszentmihalyi, 2002, p. 90): • Intense concentration• Loss of self-consciousness• A distorted sense of time. Also recognised were several features required for the initiation of flow: • A balance of high challenge and high skill • Clear and immediate feedback on a task • A sense of control over the task.

Deep Learning Model

A 2020 study by Semerci & Goularas employed a deep learning model to quantitatively measure flow in students engaged in an e-learning website. The study used skill vs. challenge from the theory of flow, and derived activity vs. performance as metrics. Activity was calculated from the following (Semerci & Goularas, 2020):

• Total amount of time a student spent after logging in • Total amount of time a student performed mouse actions • The number of mouse click actions taken by a student. • The number of in-application-locations visited by a student.

Performance was derived from quiz scores.

The Activity data was collected and converted into heatmaps, in order to show where on the screen a student is most active.

a CAE (Convolutional Autoencoder) was then used to understand the heatmaps in the following steps:

• Encoder: Downsizes the heatmap into a summary • Decoder: Mirrors the encoder with up-sampling layers to reconstruct the input, maintaining the most important features. • Result: A feature vector representing a student's activity.

The Activity vector of each student is compared to an 'Expert' vector - the vector of the most efficient and highest performing student.

tDCS Technology and a Neuroscience-based theory of flow

Transcranial Direct Current Stimulation technology can enhance or inhibit areas of the brain via anodal and/or cathodal charges, respectively. The technology has many applications in the field of neuroscience, and has been used to verify the Transient Hypofrontality Hypothesis (THH), a neuroscientific approach to a theory of flow (Dietrich, 2004).

Design


Interface Design

Flow emerges when a person’s skill level matches the challenge of a task, accompanied by immediate feedback. Neuroscience research, including the Transient Hypofrontality Hypothesis (Dietrich, 2004), suggests that modulating brain activity through tDCS can facilitate transitions to flow.

Glossary

  • Csikszentmihalyi's Flow Model (Nakamura & Csikszentmihalyi, 2002, p.95)

    The model describes flow as the balance between an individual's skill level and the difficulty of the activity. Flow occurs when both skill and challenge are high. A number of different mental states are associated with different levels of skill and challenge

  • tDCS

    tDCS (transcranial Direct Current Stimulation) is a a non-invasive, low-cost headpiece that uses low-voltage electrical currents to stimulate specific brain areas. tDCS can either enhance or inhibit brain activity using anodal or cathodal charges, respectively (Gold & Ciorciari, 2020, p. 11)

  • Flow diagram of User Experience

    While wearing the tDCS headset, the user angages with the language learning app. The deep learning algorithm in the app measures a variety of metrics in order to track whether a user is in flow or not. The app modulates its content based on these metrics.
