Learn Faster.

The Flow-Assisted Language Learning App.

A research-driven concept for a language learning platform designed to enhance learning outcomes by leveraging flow. Integrating adaptive deep learning models and tDCS technology, this project offers a novel approach to personalized, neuroscience-backed language learning.

About


What is Flow?

Flow is a psychological state of optimal performance characterised by intense focus, seamless execution, and a distorted sense of time. Originating from the work of Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, flow is a proven enhancer of skill acquisition and creativity.

Project Outline:

The following project investigates how flow can be stimulated and applied in language learning through the combined use of a gameified language learning app, an adaptive deep learning model, and tDCS (transcranial direct current stimulation) technology. The goal is to create a system that dynamically responds to user metrics, sustaining flow and enhancing language acquisition.

Motivation


Why is flow relevant to language learning?

Engagement with the flow-state has been shown to enhance cognitive performance and learning outcomes. Several modern studies have utilised tDCS to stimulate flow in the brain. • A 2021 study found a 1.5 mA cathodal right and anodal left stimulation of the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex resulted in greater language recall in foreign language learning participants. (Bolling et al., 2021) • Another successful study quantifying flow applied deep learning techniques to metrics obtained from students in an e-learning environment. (Semerci & Goularas, 2020)

Broader Implications



Thinking Cap could inform the design and direction of future learning applications, not exclusive to language learning - There could be a future where Flow-Assisted Language Learning applications are commonplace!



The following research aims to bridge neuroscience and pedagogy and synthesise such studies by:



• Developing a language learning app designed to facilitate the characteristics of flow. • Developing an adaptive deep learning model that adjusts to learners' individual needs for entering flow in real-time. • Leveraging tDCS technology to facilitate quicker transitions from explicit to implicit knowledge.

Background


The Characteristics of Flow~

Flow is colloquially referred to as being 'in the zone' or, 'locked in', and several characteristics associated with the state have been identified (Nakamura & Csikszentmihalyi, 2002, p. 90): • Intense concentration• Loss of self-consciousness• A distorted sense of time. Also recognised were several features required for the initiation of flow: • A balance of high challenge and high skill • Clear and immediate feedback on a task • A sense of control over the task.

Deep Learning Model

A 2020 study by Semerci & Goularas employed a deep learning model to quantitatively measure flow in students engaged in an e-learning website. The study used skill vs. challenge from the theory of flow, and derived activity vs. performance as metrics. Activity was calculated from the following (Semerci & Goularas, 2020):

• Total amount of time a student spent after logging in • Total amount of time a student performed mouse actions • The number of mouse click actions taken by a student. • The number of in-application-locations visited by a student.

Performance was derived from quiz scores.

The Activity data was collected and converted into heatmaps, in order to show where on the screen a student is most active.

a CAE (Convolutional Autoencoder) was then used to understand the heatmaps in the following steps:

• Encoder: Downsizes the heatmap into a summary • Decoder: Mirrors the encoder with up-sampling layers to reconstruct the input, maintaining the most important features. • Result: A feature vector representing a student's activity.

The Activity vector of each student is compared to an 'Expert' vector - the vector of the most efficient and highest performing student.

tDCS Technology and a Neuroscience-based theory of flow

Transcranial Direct Current Stimulation technology can enhance or inhibit areas of the brain via anodal and/or cathodal charges, respectively. The technology has many applications in the field of neuroscience, and has been used to verify the Transient Hypofrontality Hypothesis (THH), a neuroscientific approach to a theory of flow (Dietrich, 2004).

THH posits flow as a down-regulation of the prefrontal cortex which allows external processes to shift from explicit ot implicit knowledge, thus enabling flow.

Prefrontal Cortex: Area of brain governing self-consciousness and explicit processing. • Explicit process: An activity requiring conscious recollection of how it is done.. • Implicit process: An activity which is unconscious..

A study by Gold and Ciorciari (2019) achieved flow and found evidence for THH in a video-game based activity by applying anodal stimulation of the right parietal lobe (non-verbal memory) and cathodal stimulation of the left frontal lobe (deactivating the medial prefrontal cortex to promote faster transitions of novel information from explicit to implicit knowledge)

A similar study which inverted the anodal and cathodal stimulation achieved flow and greater language recall in left-handed foreign language learners (Bolling et al., 2021).

Design


Software

The application is designed according to the features of flow.

Balance of Challenge and Skill: • Users of Thinking Cap, the flow-assisted language learning (FALL) app, will first perform a calibration quiz in their target foreign-language, in order to measure their current level of skill, and begin calculating an optimal level of challenge. • As a user plays, the FALL app will adjust its content (task type, difficulty, frequency) according to the deep learning algorithm. • A deep learning model will create a heatmap of user Activity based on a set of metrics similar to the study by Semerci & Goularas, however adjusted for mobile-compatibility (taps instead of clicks etc..) • The deep learning model will compare the resultant vector (Activity + Accuracy) to an 'Expert' - a vector of optimal performance adjusted to their skill level, which a user should be performing at should they be in a state of flow. As the skill level of the user increases, the 'Expert' is updated accordingly.

Clear and Immediate Feedback: • The app informs the user immediately of any errors or successes, and should an error occur, the user is also informed of what went wrong.

A Sense of Control Over the Task: • The user is given autonomy over which styles of learning they are doing, whether it be reading, listening or speaking; and within those categories the type of task is chosen by the user, e.g. reading comprehension, vocab memorisation, fill-in-the-gap etc.

Hardware

• During play, a user wears a user-friendly tDCS adjusted to 1.5 mA, as this was the electrical current found most effective in Bolling et al. (2021). The purpose of the tDCS is to speed up the transition of explicit to implicit knowledge, thus speeding up language learning. • The tDCS applies cathodal stimulation to the left frontal lobe, and anodal stimulation to the right parietal lobe, as in Gold and Ciorciari (2019). Should the user be left-handed, the anodal and cathodal stimulation is swapped.

Evaluation


How Can the Success of Thinking Cap be Evaluated?

There are two variables which can be identified in Thinking Cap: The deep learning model and the tDCS technology. A thorough evaluation of the effectiveness of Thinking Cap involves isolating those variables.

• Control Experiment: Language learning is tested on a version of the app which does not utilise the deep learning model, and no tDCS is used.

• No tDCS: Language learning is tested with the deep learning model, however no tDCS is used.

• No Deep Learning Model: Language learning is tested without the deep learning model but with the tDCS

• Both: Language learning is tested with both the deep learning model and tDCS.

In each experiment a pre-test, post-test and delayed post-test on language-retention are used to measure effectiveness

Glossary

  • Csikszentmihalyi's Flow Model (Nakamura & Csikszentmihalyi, 2002, p.95)

    The model describes flow as the balance between an individual's skill level and the difficulty of the activity. Flow occurs when both skill and challenge are high. A number of different mental states are associated with different levels of skill and challenge

  • tDCS

    tDCS (transcranial Direct Current Stimulation) is a a non-invasive, low-cost headpiece that uses low-voltage electrical currents to stimulate specific brain areas. tDCS can either enhance or inhibit brain activity using anodal or cathodal charges, respectively (Gold & Ciorciari, 2020, p. 11)

  • Flow diagram of User Experience

    While wearing the tDCS headset, the user engages with the language learning app. The deep learning algorithm in the app measures a variety of metrics in order to track whether a user is in flow or not. The app modulates its content based on these metrics.

  • Evaluation Tests

    Matrix of possible experiments which must be performed in order to truly evaluate the effectiveness of Thinking Cap. a pre-test, post-test and delayed post-test on language learning abilities is performed in all cases.

  • Flow Learning Loop (Egbert, 2003, p. 500)

    Diagram detailing how flow can affect learning, which can raise skill level, and create a continuous feedback loop.
